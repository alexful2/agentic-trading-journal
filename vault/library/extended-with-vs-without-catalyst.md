# Extended Without Catalyst vs. Extended With Catalyst

"Extended" is not a single signal. Two situations look identical on a chart and
call for opposite actions. The chart cannot tell them apart; only the underlying
business change can.

**Extended without a fresh thesis-confirming catalyst** = noise. FOMO after a
run. The right response is wait, don't chase.

**Extended with a fresh thesis-confirming catalyst** = repricing. The thesis just
got stronger and the price now reflects the new thesis. The right question is "is
the new thesis worth owning at the new price?" — and if yes, paying up is
rational, not FOMO.

## Worked example (illustrative)

Imagine a fuel-cell supplier trading at $220 and widely read as "extended,
foolish to chase." But a week earlier it had signed a multi-year hyperscaler
offtake deal — a fact that did not exist before the announcement. A plan to "wait
for the dip back to $135–150" is anchored on the *pre-deal* thesis. If the stock
runs to ~$300 and the dip never comes, the mistake wasn't impatience — it was
misclassifying "extended-with-catalyst" as "extended-without-catalyst." When the
business has *materially* changed, the old entry levels are stale by construction.

## Companion principle

This pairs with [[catalyst-payoff-shape-sizing]]:

- **Catalyst-payoff-shape sizing** = how to size *into* a catalyst (asymmetric
  upside with a clean floor → cap adds; symmetric or left-skewed → cap holds).
- **Extended-with-vs-without-catalyst** = what to do in the *aftermath* of a
  catalyst (was the move repricing or noise — and only the underlying business
  change distinguishes them).

Together they cover pre- and post-event behavior. Neither is sufficient alone.

## Operational rule

When tempted to dismiss a sharp run as "extended, don't chase," explicitly
answer: **what fact about the business changed in the last 7–14 days?** If a clear
thesis-confirming fact exists, the FOMO frame is wrong — re-derive entry geometry
from the new thesis. If no such fact exists, the FOMO frame is right.
