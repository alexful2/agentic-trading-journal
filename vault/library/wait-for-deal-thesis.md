# Wait-for-Deal Thesis

> **Example framework.** This is an illustration of what a *sector-level thesis*
> looks like in this system — a personal, opinionated bet written down so the
> skills can apply it by name. It reflects one investor's view of AI
> infrastructure circa 2026; treat it as a worked example of the *form*, not a
> recommendation. Write your own for the themes you actually have conviction in.

Basic idea:

- Identify a small-to-mid-sized supplier of a scarce input in a booming industry
  (in the AI buildout: GPU capacity or power/energy).
- Take a position *before* a major customer signs a marquee partnership.
- Wait patiently. When demand for the scarce input is structurally short, large
  buyers will eventually contract with smaller, faster suppliers.

The bet is that demand for the scarce input is inevitable and the supply is
genuinely constrained, so a deal is a matter of *when*, not *if*.

## The advantage is temporal, not structural

The edge of most "wait-for-deal" names is being a *credible counterparty now* —
land, permits, allocation, crews already in flight. Anyone with capital can build
the same thing in 2–3 years; only a handful can deliver capacity *this* year.
That's a windowed advantage, not a moat.

Implications for how you hold these:

- **Supply/demand mismatch trade, not a compounder.** The edge has a half-life —
  it lasts until buildout catches demand and margins compress. Don't size it like
  a durable franchise.
- **Temporal vs. structural.** Temporal = early, scarce, credible-now. Structural
  = technology with real yield or durability edges that took years to
  commercialize. The more structural the edge, the more cushion — size
  accordingly.
- **Trim trigger.** When fresh partnership announcements stop moving the stock,
  the scarcity premium is gone.

## Post-deal: the framework no longer applies — run a fresh deep dive

Once the partnership lands, the wait-for-deal frame is consumed. It told you when
to *get in*, not what to do *after*. The post-deal name is a different security:
revenue is anchored, the multiple has repriced, and the relevant question becomes
whether the *post-deal multiple* is worth holding — not whether the *pre-deal
scarcity* was real. **Default action when a deal lands: run a fresh deep dive.**

Three post-deal patterns to classify which shape a name fits:

- **Immediate compression** — deal priced in within 1–2 days; stock flat-to-down
  afterward. The news is already in the price; paying up post-news doesn't work.
  Any follow-on entry comes from a normal dip, not the deal.
- **Sustained repricing** — a multi-week run as sell-side upgrades and additional
  capacity get announced and absorbed. Paying up post-news is rational here under
  [[extended-with-vs-without-catalyst]] — the price reflects the new thesis, not
  noise.
- **Sell-the-news** — gap up on announcement, then bleed back over weeks as deal
  terms get parsed (margin compression, dilution, conditionality, customer
  concentration). Waiting for the give-back is correct.

The deep dive determines which pattern applies. There is no single post-deal rule
across the cohort; the patterns differ by deal terms, balance sheet, and how
stretched the stock was going in.

## Sourcing note

The hard part is pipeline: a thesis like this is only useful if you have a
*standing list* of pre-deal candidates feeding it. Once the obvious names have
all signed, every new entry has to come from off-watchlist sourcing (micro-caps,
pre-IPO names). A weekly opportunity-radar pass is the channel for that — see how
`vault-curator` and the IPO calendar are wired in this repo.
