# C10 — Limits to Arbitrage

> Empirical-priors research note (research/ catalog). Not investment advice.

**Claim.** Real-world arbitrage is done by specialized, capital-constrained agents using other people's money, so mispricings can persist — and even *widen* — because the arbitrageurs who would correct them get pulled out exactly when the opportunity is largest.

**Sources.**
- Shleifer & Vishny (1997), "The Limits of Arbitrage," *Journal of Finance* 52(1):35–55. DOI 10.1111/j.1540-6261.1997.tb03807.x *(citation of record)*. Free copy: Stanford-hosted PDF. Predecessor: NBER WP 5167 (1995).

**Evidence setting.** A theoretical model of "performance-based arbitrage" (PBA): outside investors allocate capital to an arbitrageur as a function of *past* returns, not expected future returns. Largely conceptual — no headline empirical figure.

**Replication & decay.** Core result (verbatim): *"arbitrageurs can become most constrained when they have the best opportunities, i.e., when the mispricing they have bet against gets even worse."* In extreme circumstances arbitrageurs may be forced to liquidate "when their participation is most needed," so prices can diverge further before converging. The mechanism is a model, not a decaying empirical edge.

**What this is NOT.** NOT a license to hold any losing position. The paper itself is candid that PBA's real-world force is unsettled and that the forced-liquidation result holds specifically at *extreme* / fully-invested corner cases.

**How to apply.** This is the *intellectual justification* for two instincts: the [[Wait-for-deal thesis]] (a patient holder with non-redeemable capital can sit in small/mid names that constrained arb capital can't) and for not bailing the moment something "looks wrong." The structural edge is *plausible, not automatic*: with no redemptions, no margin calls, and no quarterly performance gate, you *can* hold through divergences that force levered funds out — **but only when the divergence is genuinely forced-selling / sentiment and the fundamentals are intact** (see the falsification below). Absent that, there is no edge here — just a losing position.

**Decision-rule translation (with the falsification up front).**
- **The trap:** "mispricing widens because the market is constrained" and "mispricing widens because I am wrong" look identical in the price. C10 only applies in the first case.
- Before invoking limits-to-arbitrage to justify a hold, write the explicit *what-would-tell-me-I'm-wrong* line. C10 applies only when (a) the thesis evidence is intact or improving, and (b) the divergence is driven by forced/constrained selling or sentiment — **not** deteriorating fundamentals. If the fundamentals are deteriorating, it's a correct repricing and you're the one who's wrong.
- Never use this note as a standalone reason to hold a loser. It is an *edge* only when paired with intact, independently-checked evidence.

**Tension / failure mode.** This is the single most dangerous note in the catalog, because it can rationalize loss aversion ([[C6 - Disposition Effect]]). The guardrail is the falsification line above: limits-to-arbitrage without a live falsification condition is just an excuse. Patience is an edge only when the thesis is right; when it's wrong, patience is the disposition effect wearing a finance-paper costume.
