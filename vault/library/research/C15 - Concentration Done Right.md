# C15 — Concentration Done Right

> Empirical-priors research note (research/ catalog). Not investment advice.

**Claim.** Concentration is rewarded *when it reflects genuine information advantage* — not mere conviction. Investors' highest-conviction "best ideas" outperform; their filler positions don't.

**Sources.**
- Ivković, Sialm & Weisbenner (2008), "Portfolio Concentration and the Performance of Individual Investors," *Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis* 43(3):613–655. DOI / NBER WP 10675. Free copy: Weisbenner author PDF (Illinois).
- Antón, Cohen & Polk (2020/2021), "Best Ideas," HBS WP 21-004 / SSRN 1364827 *(working paper)*. Lineage: Cohen, Polk & Silli (2008), FMG/LSE WP 624 (earlier mutual-fund-only version).

**Evidence setting.** *ISW:* US discount-broker households; performance of holdings/trades sliced by portfolio concentration, portfolio size, and stock type. *ACP:* US active mutual-fund and hedge-fund managers; the single highest active-weight ("best idea") stock vs. the rest of each manager's portfolio. Both empirical, US equities.

**Replication & decay.** *ISW:* concentrated households' holdings beat diversified by **~0.16 pp/month risk-adjusted (~2%/yr)** (raw 1.50% vs 1.36%/mo); the premium is **concentrated in the inefficient corners** — ~50 bps/month in non-S&P-500 stocks, ~112 bps/month in *local* non-S&P-500 stocks, and stronger for large portfolios. Small concentrated portfolios and S&P-500/well-covered names show **no** concentration premium — consistent with *information advantage*, not concentration per se. *ACP:* best-idea stocks outperform the market and the managers' other holdings by **~2.8–4.5%/year** (the 2020/21 version; the 2008 Cohen-Polk-Silli version reported 39–127 bps/month, mutual funds only); the rest of the portfolio does **not** significantly outperform.

**What this is NOT.** NOT "concentration always wins." ISW finds concentration pays *only with* information advantage — in efficient, well-covered names (mega-caps, S&P 500) there's no premium, and small under-informed concentrated accounts don't outperform. ACP is about managers' *best ideas*, and its policy point is that *filler* positions are the problem, not that any concentrated book is good.

**How to apply — the explicit counterweight to [[C9 - Most Stocks Underperform T-Bills]].** C9 is the base-rate case *against* concentration (most stocks lose to T-bills). C15 is the other half: concentration is *rewarded* when it sits on real informational edge in under-covered names — precisely the situation where a wait-for-deal / small-mid thesis lives (under-covered relative to mega-caps). Together they set the bar: **concentrate only where you have a genuine, nameable information advantage in a less-efficient name.**

**Decision-rule translation.**
- A concentrated bet is justified only if you can *name the information advantage* (an execution-mosaic read, a deal-timing edge — not "I like the story") **and** it lives in a less-efficient corner (under-covered, non-mega-cap). That's where ISW's premium actually is.
- An edge concentrates in private mosaic work (`execution-thesis` / `projects.md`) on under-covered names — size those as best ideas. Mega-cap, efficiently-covered names carry **no concentration premium** — size them as beta exposure, not "best ideas."
- **Cull filler (ACP):** any position you can't write a best-idea case for should be small or gone — it's the un-outperforming remainder, not an edge.

**Tension / failure mode.** This is the seductive note — "information advantage" is trivial to *claim* and hard to *have*. ISW's premium required **real** asymmetry (local, under-covered, low-analyst names); don't launder conviction into "information advantage." The honest test: would the edge survive being written down and checked by an adversary (`execution-audit`)? If not, you're in C9's base-rate-punished majority, not C15's informed minority. C15 does not loosen C9 — it *narrows the gate* concentration must pass.
