# research/ — empirical-priors catalog

A small catalog (C1–C15) that distills foundational, peer-reviewed finance
findings into **decision priors**: short notes that translate a paper's result
into a rule you can actually apply at the desk.

These cover broadly-applicable results — arbitrage decay, the factor replication
crisis, post-earnings drift, momentum, profitability/quality, the disposition
effect, overtrading, Kelly sizing, "most stocks underperform T-bills," limits to
arbitrage, low-beta, lottery stocks, asset growth, share issuance, and
concentration. They apply to almost any equity approach, not one strategy.

## What this layer is — and isn't

- **It's a calibration layer, not an alpha source.** A published, public edge is
  already partly arbitraged by the time you read it (that's literally C1). The
  job of these notes is to keep your *own* reasoning honest — to stop you from
  over-betting an uncalibrated edge, chasing a dead factor, or mistaking a
  lottery sleeve for a core position. Your alpha, if any, is your private
  research; these are the guardrails around it.
- **It's a starter set, not the canon.** This catalog was assembled to match one
  investor's strategy. The genuinely transferable thing isn't the exact 15 — it's
  the *pattern*: find the papers that bear on how *you* actually trade, read the
  primary sources, and distill each into a prior you'll remember. Build your own
  as your vault matures; add, cut, and re-weight to fit your book.

## The verify-then-stamp discipline

Every note follows the same skeleton, and the part that makes it trustworthy is
the last line:

```
Claim · Sources · Evidence setting · Replication & decay ·
What this is NOT · How to apply · Decision-rule translation ·
Tension / failure mode · Links · Verification
```

**Verification** records when the claim was last checked against the *primary
source*, and whether every figure matched (`CONFIRMED` / `PARTIAL`, with a date).
This is the discipline that keeps a "research note" from quietly drifting into a
half-remembered statistic. If you add your own priors, hold them to the same bar:
cite the paper, check the numbers yourself, and stamp the date.

## How the skills use these

`stock-deep-dive` pulls the relevant priors into its reasoning (e.g. C8/Kelly for
sizing, C12 for lottery-like names, C3 for post-earnings drift). They inform the
verdict; they don't mechanically drive it. Nothing here is investment advice.
