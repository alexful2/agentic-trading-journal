# C1 — Publication & Arbitrage Decay

> Empirical-priors research note (research/ catalog). Not investment advice.

**Claim.** Even genuine published return predictors lose most of their edge out-of-sample, and lose more again once published — the act of making an edge public erodes it.

**Sources.**
- McLean & Pontiff (2016), "Does Academic Research Destroy Stock Return Predictability?", *Journal of Finance* 71(1):5–31. DOI 10.1111/jofi.12365 *(citation of record)*. Free copies: SSRN 2156623; author PDF.

**Evidence setting.** 97 cross-sectional return predictors drawn from 80 published studies; each compared across three windows — (i) the original in-sample period, (ii) post-sample but pre-publication, (iii) post-publication. US equities, long-short extreme-quintile portfolios. Portfolio-level — **not** single-stock.

**Replication & decay.** Average long-short return: in-sample 0.582%/mo → out-of-sample 0.402%/mo (**−26%**, an *upper bound* on data-mining/statistical bias) → post-publication 0.264%/mo (**−58%**). The publication-specific effect is 58% − 26% = **32%**. Decay is *larger* for predictors that are cheaper to arbitrage (liquid, low-idiosyncratic-risk stocks) and for those with higher in-sample returns; predictor-portfolio cross-correlations rise after publication. The authors statistically reject both "predictability vanishes entirely" and "no post-publication change."

**What this is NOT.** NOT "all anomalies are fake" — that's the never-replicated set ([[C2 - Replication Crisis in Factor Anomalies]]). This is the narrower, sharper point: the half-life of a *public, real* edge is short. Not a single-name signal.

**How to apply.** Treat this as the **second gate**. A discretionary holder reading public papers and public news is downstream of arbitrage capital: anything you can read, arbitrage capital already has — and the *more liquid* the name (the ones you actually trade), the faster the edge is competed away. So treat every imported finding as already partly arbitraged before acting on it.

**Decision-rule translation.**
- Discount any published edge by default; the older the publication, the harder the discount (a 2009 anomaly is more arbitraged than a 2022 one).
- Your durable edges are **structural** (patient, non-redeemable capital — [[C10 - Limits to Arbitrage]]) or **informational** (private mosaic/execution work not yet public — `company-projects` / `execution-thesis`), **not** factor replication you read about.
- This reframes the entire layer: these notes are **calibration priors, not alpha sources.** The alpha is your private research.

**Tension / failure mode.** Decay ≠ disappearance — McLean-Pontiff explicitly reject *full* disappearance, and structural limits to arbitrage ([[C10 - Limits to Arbitrage]]) let edges like momentum and value persist for decades. Don't use "it's published, so it's dead" to dismiss a still-working prior. This is the opposite error from C2's nihilism.
