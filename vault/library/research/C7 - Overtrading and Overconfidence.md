# C7 — Overtrading & Overconfidence

> Empirical-priors research note (research/ catalog). Not investment advice.

**Claim.** Individual investors' stock *selection* is roughly average, but their *trading frequency* — driven by overconfidence — turns ordinary gross returns into materially below-market net returns. The most active traders fare worst.

**Sources.**
- Barber & Odean (2000), "Trading Is Hazardous to Your Wealth: The Common Stock Investment Performance of Individual Investors," *Journal of Finance* 55(2):773–806. DOI 10.1111/0022-1082.00226 *(citation of record)*. Free copy: author PDF, faculty.haas.berkeley.edu.
- Mechanism companion (not separately verified here): Barber & Odean (2001), "Boys Will Be Boys: Gender, Overconfidence, and Common Stock Investment," *QJE* 116(1) — links the excess trading to overconfidence.

**Evidence setting.** 66,465 households at a large discount broker, 1991–1996 (returns Feb 1991–Jan 1997). Households sorted by portfolio turnover; net and gross returns compared to the value-weighted NYSE/AMEX/Nasdaq index.

**Replication & decay.** Most-active turnover quintile earned **11.4%** net annualized vs **18.5%** for the least active; the market index returned **17.9%**. The average household earned **18.7% gross / 16.4% net**. Gross returns are statistically indistinguishable from the market — *stock-picking is ~average* — so the **net** shortfall is almost entirely trading costs × turnover (most-active quintile turned over >250%/yr). Verbatim: "It is the cost of trading and the frequency of trading, not portfolio selections, that explain the poor investment performance of households."

**What this is NOT.** NOT "individuals can't pick stocks" — their gross picks roughly matched the market. NOT an argument against *holding* equities. It's specifically an indictment of *churn*.

**How to apply.** This is the uncomfortable counterweight to any self-improvement apparatus — reviews, curation, journaling, and a research layer like this one. The machinery has to make you trade *better*, not *more*. A system that surfaces more "actionable" items every day can quietly manufacture turnover, which Barber-Odean says is the single most reliable way for a retail account to underperform.

**Decision-rule translation.**
- Treat turnover as a cost to be justified, not a sign of diligence. The default action on most alerts is *no trade*.
- Periodically check whether the tooling is raising trade count without raising returns — if so, the tooling is net-negative regardless of how good each note reads.
- Because gross selection ≈ market on average, a realistic edge lives in (a) *not* churning and (b) private informational work (`company-projects` / `execution-thesis`), not in trading more often on public signals.

**Tension / failure mode.** This is the *retail-average* failure mode. A concentrated, patient holder who trades rarely is closer to the *opposite* profile — the live risk there is an apparatus tempting more action, not the holding itself. Don't weaponize this note into "never sell" either: legitimate thesis breaks and [[opportunity-cost lens]] rotations are not "overtrading."
