# C5b — Quality Minus Junk (Quality Compounder Checklist)

> Empirical-priors research note (research/ catalog). Not investment advice.

**Claim.** Stocks that are more profitable, faster-growing, and safer ("quality") earn higher risk-adjusted returns than "junk" — and the market prices quality only modestly, so quality is, on average, relatively underpriced.

**Sources.**
- Asness, Frazzini & Pedersen (2019), "Quality Minus Junk," *Review of Accounting Studies* 24(1):34–112. DOI 10.1007/s11142-018-9470-2 *(citation of record; gold open access — the version of record is itself free)*. Working paper: SSRN 2312432.

**Evidence setting.** "Quality" is built from three pillars — **profitability, growth, safety** (with a payout/management component added in some specifications) — motivated by a Gordon-growth identity: justified P/B = (profitability × payout) / (required return − growth). US 1957–2016; 24 developed markets 1989–2016. QMJ = long high-quality, short junk.

**Replication & decay.** High-minus-low quality decile returns of **42–52 bps/month** (t = 2.56, 2.49), with risk-adjusted returns "much larger." The QMJ factor's abnormal returns: **US 39/51/60 bps/mo** (CAPM/3-factor/4-factor; t = 5.43/8.90/9.95) and **global 51/61/61 bps/mo** (t = 5.76/8.75/8.07), information ratio > 1, positive in **23 of 24 countries**. QMJ has *negative* market/value/size exposure and benefits from flight-to-quality. Introduces **QARP** ("quality at a reasonable price") — a low price-of-quality predicts high future QMJ returns.

**What this is NOT.** NOT a single-name signal, and NOT the same as C5a — C5a is one clean accounting ratio; C5b is a *composite definition of a good business*. It is a diversified long-short factor; it does not say "buy any quality stock at any price" (hence QARP).

**How to apply.** Treat this as the **compounder checklist**. [[C9 - Most Stocks Underperform T-Bills]] says concentration only pays if you're in the genuine compounders; QMJ operationalizes what "genuine compounder" means — profitable, growing, safe, well-managed. Use it as the rubric when judging whether a concentrated bet deserves to exist.

**Decision-rule translation.**
- Score a candidate/holding on the three pillars (profitable? growing? safe — low leverage, low earnings volatility, low bankruptcy risk?). A name that fails all three is "junk" and needs an extraordinary thesis to hold at size.
- Apply QARP: quality is worth paying *up* for, but not infinitely — a high price-of-quality (everyone crowding into quality) is when QMJ returns are weakest. Don't pay any price for a great business.
- Pair with [[C5a - Gross Profitability Premium]] (the single-ratio version) and [[C8 - Kelly Criterion and Position Sizing]] (size the highest-quality convictions larger, within fractional-Kelly limits).

**Tension / failure mode.** "Safety" penalizes exactly the high-beta, capital-intensive, volatile-earnings names that often anchor a sharp-exposure thesis ([[C11 - Betting Against Beta]] reinforces this). So QMJ will rate an edgier sleeve as low-quality — that's a *feature* (it flags the real risk), but it is not a sell signal: a deliberate power-law-upside bet is not a quality bet. Hold the two frames separately rather than letting QMJ override a deliberate speculative position.
