# C6 — Disposition Effect

> Empirical-priors research note (research/ catalog). Not investment advice.

**Claim.** Investors sell winners far more readily than losers — the opposite of what tax efficiency and the evidence on subsequent returns would dictate.

**Sources.**
- Odean (1998), "Are Investors Reluctant to Realize Their Losses?", *Journal of Finance* 53(5):1775–1798. DOI 10.1111/0022-1082.00072 *(citation of record)*. Free copy: author PDF, faculty.haas.berkeley.edu.

**Evidence setting.** 10,000 accounts at a large nationwide discount broker, 1987–1993 (162,948 trades; 1,258,135 positions). Measures the Proportion of Gains Realized (PGR) vs the Proportion of Losses Realized (PLR) — i.e., conditional on having a paper gain/loss, how often it's actually sold.

**Replication & decay.** Full year: **PGR = 0.148 vs PLR = 0.098** (winners realized ~1.5× as readily as losers; difference −0.050, null PGR≤PLR rejected at t > 35). Jan–Nov: PGR 0.152 vs PLR 0.094. **December fully reverses** (PGR 0.108 < PLR 0.128) as tax-loss selling kicks in. Crucially, the sold winners *subsequently outperform* the held losers — so the behavior is not explained by rebalancing, transaction-cost avoidance, or superior information. It's a behavioral error.

**What this is NOT.** Not a market-level anomaly you trade — it's a description of an *investor* tendency (yours included). Not a claim that holding any given position is wrong; it's a claim about the *systematic direction* of the error.

**How to apply.** This is the empirical backbone of two recurring instincts: the "give it a little more time" reflex (often loss aversion in disguise) and the [[opportunity-cost lens]]. Odean's kicker — held losers underperformed sold winners — is the direct indictment: the instinct to hold the red position is exactly the move the data says destroys return.

**Decision-rule translation.**
- When tempted to "give it more time" on a down position, treat it as a disposition-effect alarm and run the [[opportunity-cost lens]] (holding = re-buying at today's price).
- Symmetrically: a *winner* you feel itchy to trim "to lock it in" is the same bias mirrored — check whether the thesis actually changed before selling strength.
- The bias is wrong-signed by default; the burden of proof is on the hold-the-loser / sell-the-winner instinct.

**Tension / failure mode.** This is an *average* retail tendency, not a verdict on every hold. A thesis that is genuinely intact while price diverges is not the disposition effect — it may be [[C10 - Limits to Arbitrage]] in action. The discipline is to distinguish "I'm holding because I can't face the loss" (disposition) from "I'm holding because the evidence still supports it and constrained capital is mispricing it" (C10). If you can't write the latter case explicitly, assume it's the former.
