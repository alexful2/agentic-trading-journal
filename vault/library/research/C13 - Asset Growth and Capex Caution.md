# C13 — Asset Growth & Capex Caution

> Empirical-priors research note (research/ catalog). Not investment advice.

**Claim.** Firms that aggressively expand their asset base — high asset growth, ramped capital investment, equity issuance — subsequently *underperform*. Investors overpay for expansion ("empire building") and are disappointed.

**Sources.**
- Cooper, Gulen & Schill (2008), "Asset Growth and the Cross-Section of Stock Returns," *Journal of Finance* 63(4):1609–1651. DOI 10.1111/j.1540-6261.2008.01370.x *(citation of record)*. SSRN 760967.
- Titman, Wei & Xie (2004), "Capital Investments and Stock Returns," *Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis* 39(4):677–700. DOI 10.1017/S0022109000003173. Free copy: NBER WP 9951.

**Evidence setting.** *Cooper-Gulen-Schill:* sort on year-over-year % change in total assets, US equities, 1968–2003. *Titman-Wei-Xie:* sort on *abnormal* capital investment (capex/sales vs the firm's prior 3-year average), 1973–1996. Both cross-sectional, long-low/short-high.

**Replication & decay.** *Asset growth:* low-growth decile beat high-growth decile by a risk-adjusted **~20%/yr equal-weighted (19.5%)** and **~8%/yr value-weighted (8.4%)**; raw VW returns ran ~18%/yr (low) vs ~5%/yr (high), monotonic across deciles, t-stats >2× those of other known predictors. *Capex:* the low-minus-high abnormal-investment spread earns **~2%/yr** benchmark-adjusted (0.168%/mo, t=2.91; ~2.3%/yr four-factor), persisting ~5 years, concentrated in high-cash-flow / low-debt firms with the *discretion* to overinvest.

**What this is NOT.** Cross-sectional averages — **NOT** single-name sell signals, and NOT a claim that all capex is bad. The capex effect specifically isolates *abnormal* investment relative to a firm's own history; the asset-growth effect is broader (total assets, including acquisitions and issuance).

**How to apply — a book-specific note.** A sleeve full of capital-intensive, aggressively-building, equity-issuing names is the sharpest case here. These two anomalies say that profile — big asset growth, ramped capex, dilution — is a *structural headwind* on the empirical base rate. That doesn't kill a thesis; it sets the bar: each name needs a specific reason it's the exception to "expansion gets punished."

**Decision-rule translation.**
- Treat aggressive buildout as a *yellow flag against the base rate*, not a green light. The bull case has to clear it explicitly.
- The exception condition is **demand-matched, contracted, visible capex** (offtake/PPAs/signed capacity) vs speculative overbuild. The first is NPV-positive expansion the market underrates; the second is exactly what the anomaly punishes.
- This is where private mosaic work pays off: `company-projects` and `execution-thesis` exist precisely to verify whether a name's buildout is demand-matched or empire-building. That verification is how you clear C13 — and it ties back to [[C1 - Publication & Arbitrage Decay]]'s punchline that your edge is private execution research, not the public factor.
- Watch dilution as part of the mechanism (net equity issuance predicts underperformance too).

**Tension / failure mode.** A buildout thesis usually rests on the claim that *this* capex is value-creating because demand is real and contracted — i.e., these names are the exception. That may well be true, but it's also exactly what every overbuilding management claims. The anomaly is the base rate; the `execution-thesis` mosaic is the only honest way to decide whether a specific name beats it. Don't let conviction substitute for the verification.
