# Opportunity-Cost Lens

Basic idea: the sell decision should be reframed as a comparative buy decision.

Every day you hold a stock, you are implicitly re-buying it at today's price. If
another stock in your portfolio or watchlist is a clearly better buy at its
current price, continuing to hold the first one is the same as choosing the worse
trade. Ownership shouldn't get a discount — "I already own it" is not a reason.

Example: you bought a position at $37. It's now $43 and you believe it can reach
$50+. Even so, the right question isn't "can it go higher?" It's "at $43, is this
a better deal than the next-best name on my watchlist at its current price?" If
the answer is no, the decision to hold needs a non-price reason (e.g. an imminent
catalyst).

Key refinements:

- **Discrete catalysts distort pure relative-value math.** If a stock has a
  binary earnings event in 20 days, part of what you own is optionality on that
  date. Comparing it to a name without a near-term catalyst isn't
  apples-to-apples. The right move there is often to wait for the catalyst — but
  that's the exception, not the rule.
- **Partial rotations are the cleanest way to apply this.** You don't have to
  choose between "hold everything" and "sell everything." If name B is marginally
  better than name A but A has a catalyst, trimming some A into B honors both
  views.
- **Concentration is its own signal.** If a position is 80%+ of the portfolio,
  the opportunity-cost lens matters more, because binary risk is concentrated.
  Even at equal expected return, spreading capital across two high-conviction
  names beats doubling down on one.
- **Don't prioritize a holding just because you own it.** This is the emotional
  trap. "I'll give it a little more time" is fine only if the position is
  actually the best buy at current prices. Otherwise it's loss aversion dressed
  up as patience.

Connects to the [[Wait-for-deal thesis]]: deal-driven names often become *less*
attractive after the deal is announced and the stock runs — the opportunity-cost
lens catches that transition earlier than "let winners run" thinking does.
