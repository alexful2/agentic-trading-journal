# The Lot Line: Permitting and Political Consent as Binding Constraints

[[AI Energy Thesis]] names *power* as the binding constraint. This entry adds the variable one layer up: even where power, water, and land exist, **political consent** can block a data center, and political consent cannot be purchased at any price.

> "Data centers need four things money cannot buy: land, water, power, political consent."

**Empirical anchor:** Tens of billions of dollars of U.S. data-center projects have been blocked or delayed by community opposition in recent years; a large share of projects facing sustained local opposition are eventually canceled. State-level bans (e.g. Maine LD 307 banning new data centers above 20MW) and local moratoria are now spreading faster than power-generation capacity is being built.

## How this constraint translates into a portfolio tilt

Names with *already-permitted* land, power, and grid interconnect are structurally long this constraint — every new moratorium passed somewhere else makes their existing footprint scarcer. Names that still need to *get* permits in contested jurisdictions face a discrete risk layer that doesn't show up in capex models.

- **Already-permitted (long the constraint):** operators whose flagship campuses already hold land + power + interconnect.
- **Still permit-dependent (short the constraint):** expansion campuses awaiting approval; greenfield on-site generation at *new* customer sites, where local backlash to data-center power loads can sweep in the on-site generation too.

When a moratorium headline lands, the right move is to map it onto the specific footprints in the cohort rather than reading it as generic AI-infra bearishness.

## Don't over-index — the macro overrides

Permitting is a real constraint at the *individual-project level*. At the macro level, the overall AI demand curve is likely to overwhelm regulatory friction, and under sufficient geopolitical pressure (US–China rivalry over compute), federal preemption of local permitting is more plausible than not. The multi-year narrative is "we built it anyway"; the near-term narrative is "the names that didn't have to wait for a permit got paid for not waiting."

The right framing is therefore:
- **Macro thesis: unchanged.** AI gets built. Don't trim the cohort because of one moratorium.
- **Intra-cohort tilt: real.** Pre-permitted footprints earn a scarcity premium right now. Permit-pending sites carry a discrete risk layer the model should price.
